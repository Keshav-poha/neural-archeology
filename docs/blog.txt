Neural Archaeology: Digging for Hidden Fingerprints in Smartphone Camera Sensors
==================================================================================
A plain-English walkthrough of the research

---

INTRODUCTION: WHAT IS THIS ALL ABOUT?
--------------------------------------

Every camera sensor has a personality. Even before you aim it at anything, even in
total darkness, it produces a faint, repeating pattern of light and dark pixels that
has nothing to do with the scene in front of it. This pattern is baked into the
hardware. It is called Fixed-Pattern Noise, or FPN.

The question this research asks is: can we reliably detect and measure these
sensor "fingerprints" from real-world footage? And more importantly, are these
fingerprints consistent across different scenes, or do they change depending on
what you are filming?

To answer this, we used the AIM 2025 Low-Light RAW Video Denoising dataset. It
was captured by a group of researchers at Lomonosov Moscow State University who
mounted 14 different smartphone cameras on a motorised rail inside a dark photo
box, lit only by a carefully controlled LED lamp set to roughly 1 lux — about the
brightness of a single candle from a metre away. They filmed five different
tabletop scenes (coloured objects, hardware parts, springs, toys, yarn) under
these extreme conditions.

The key insight behind the dataset: because the cameras were mounted on a rail
and shot in stop-motion (move, shoot, move, shoot), every frame is precisely
aligned. There is no camera shake to confuse the analysis. That alignment is what
makes it possible to separate true scene signal from sensor artefacts.

Each sensor produced its data as a .npz file — a compressed dictionary containing
the 10 main noisy frames, 20 extra-noisy frames of the final position, and a
ground-truth frame built by averaging 200 of those extra-noisy frames to wash out
random noise and leave only the true scene signal.

Our job: take that raw data and systematically excavate the sensor fingerprints
buried inside it.


THE CAMERAS
-----------

Fourteen camera modules were used, spread across five smartphones:

  Samsung Galaxy Z Fold4   — wide, ultrawide, and telephoto cameras
  Google Pixel 5a          — wide, ultrawide, and front cameras
  Google Pixel 7 Pro       — wide, ultrawide, telephoto, and front cameras
  Samsung Galaxy S20       — wide and ultrawide cameras
  POCO X3 Pro              — wide and front cameras

They cover a wide range of sensor generations, sizes, and optical designs,
which is exactly what makes this dataset interesting. If you can find consistent
fingerprints even across sensors this different, you have learned something real.


THE DATA FORMAT
---------------

Each .npz file contains floating-point 2D arrays (one array per frame) where 0.0
means "no light detected" and 1.0 means "sensor fully saturated". The images are
still in RAW format — meaning they have not been processed by any camera software.
They still look like a chequerboard of red, green, and blue pixels because the
Bayer filter (the colour filter array sitting on top of the sensor) has not been
removed. We deliberately left the data in this form to avoid any processing that
might wash away or alter the fingerprints we are looking for.


---

STEP 1: GATHERING THE NUMBERS (compute_statistics.py)
------------------------------------------------------

Before we can look for a fingerprint, we need to measure the data carefully.

For every sensor in every scene, we load all 10 noisy frames and compute
statistics at the level of individual pixels — not the whole image at once.
That means for each pixel, we ask: across the 10 frames, what is the average
brightness? How much does it bounce around (variance)? How spread out are the
values (standard deviation)?

We use Welford's online algorithm to do this. The reason is memory: a full-size
RAW image from these cameras is about 40 megapixels. Loading all 10 frames at
the same time into RAM and then computing statistics would require several
gigabytes. Welford's algorithm lets you update the mean and variance one frame at
a time, throwing away the previous frame once it is processed, so the memory
footprint stays tiny.

We do the same calculation on the 20 extra-noisy frames separately. These extra
frames were captured at the same position as the final frame but with the sensor
pushed even harder into noise. They tell us how the sensor behaves at extreme
noise levels, which is useful for checking whether our fingerprint measurements
are stable across different noise conditions.

The output for each sensor/scene pair is a small .npz file containing:
  - The pixel-by-pixel temporal mean (what the sensor "expects" to see on average)
  - The pixel-by-pixel variance and standard deviation
  - A copy of the ground-truth frame
  - Per-frame global brightness and spread values
  - The same stats computed from the extra-noisy frames

Think of this step as taking a careful measurement before doing any real analysis.
We are not interpreting anything yet — just counting.


---

STEP 2: EXTRACTING THE FINGERPRINT (extract_bias.py)
-----------------------------------------------------

Now we use those statistics to extract what we actually care about.

The BIAS MAP for a sensor/scene pair is simply the temporal mean image computed
in Step 1. When you average 10 noisy frames together, the random noise partially
cancels out (random fluctuations above and below zero average toward zero), but
the fixed pattern does not cancel — it stays. The result is an image that mostly
shows the sensor's persistent, scene-independent noise.

The FPN RESIDUAL goes one step further. We subtract the ground-truth frame from
the bias map:

    FPN residual = bias map − ground truth

The ground truth tells us what the true scene looks like (with random noise
averaged away). Whatever is left after subtraction is the systematic offset that
the sensor adds on top of the real signal. Non-zero values here mean the sensor
is adding its own signature to every pixel.

We also compute two simplified versions:

  Row FPN: average the entire bias map across columns, giving one value per row.
  This captures horizontal banding — stripes that run left to right across the
  whole image, caused by the way rows of pixels share readout circuitry.

  Column FPN: the same idea but averaged across rows, capturing vertical banding.

SCENE INDEPENDENCE TEST

Here is the critical question: is this fingerprint actually a property of the
sensor, or does it change scene to scene?

To test this, we take each sensor's bias map from all 5 scenes and compute
Pearson correlation (r) between every possible pair of scenes. The Pearson r
measures how similar two patterns are, from -1 (opposite) to 0 (unrelated) to
+1 (identical).

If a sensor's fingerprint is a true sensor property, you would expect the bias
maps from different scenes to look very similar — their r values should be close
to 1. If the fingerprint is mostly scene content bleeding through (because 10
frames is not enough to wash away all noise), r would be lower.

We save a 5×5 correlation matrix per sensor and compute the mean off-diagonal
value as a single summary number.


---

STEP 3: COMPARING SENSORS AND FINDING STRUCTURE (analyse_bias.py)
------------------------------------------------------------------

With 14 sensors each having their own fingerprint, the next question is: how
similar are the sensors to each other?

PAIRWISE SIMILARITY

We compute Pearson r between every possible pair of sensor bias maps. Because
sensors have different native resolutions (the Galaxy Z Fold4 Telephoto shoots at
3648×2736, the Pixel 7 Pro at 4080×3072, and so on), we cannot directly compare
raw images — they are different sizes. So we first scale every bias map down by a
factor of 20 (turning a 3648×2736 image into a ~182×137 thumbnail) and then crop
to the smallest common size. This shrinking also speeds up computation enormously.

The result is a 14×14 similarity matrix: each cell tells you how similar sensor A's
fingerprint is to sensor B's, averaged across all scenes.

HIERARCHICAL CLUSTERING

A 14×14 table is hard to read at a glance. Clustering organises the sensors into
a tree (called a dendrogram) based on how similar their fingerprints are.

We use Ward linkage, which builds the tree by repeatedly merging the two groups
whose merger would increase the total within-group spread the least. Sensors with
very similar fingerprints end up on the same branch, sensors with very different
fingerprints end up on distant branches.

This tells us, for example, whether all sensors from the same phone tend to cluster
together (suggesting the fingerprint is mainly driven by the readout chip shared
across modules on one phone) or whether the clustering follows some other pattern
(like sensor physical size, or manufacturer).

POWER SPECTRAL DENSITY (PSD)

A fingerprint might contain periodic structure — regular stripes or patterns that
repeat at a fixed frequency. Power Spectral Density analysis reveals this.

We apply a standard Fourier transform to each row of the bias map and measure how
much energy is present at each spatial frequency. We do the same along each column.
Then we average across all rows (and all columns) to get a smooth summary curve.

A spike in the PSD curve means there is a regular repeating pattern at that spatial
frequency. A flat curve means the noise is more random. Comparing PSD curves across
sensors can reveal whether different sensors share common periodic artefacts (which
might come from the readout electronics) or whether their noise structures are
independent.

FPN MAGNITUDE SUMMARY

Finally, we compute a single number per sensor: the root-mean-square (RMS) of its
FPN residual. RMS is like an "average strength" of the fingerprint — a higher RMS
means a stronger, more visible fixed pattern.

We compute this per sensor per scene, then aggregate across scenes to get:
  - Mean RMS FPN (how strong the fingerprint is overall)
  - Variance of RMS FPN across scenes (how much the strength varies by scene)

Low variance here supports our hypothesis that the fingerprint is a stable sensor
property, not something that varies with scene content.

STEP 4: TURNING RESULTS INTO PICTURES (visualise.py)
-----------------------------------------------------

Numbers in .npz files are hard to communicate. This step converts everything into
images and charts.

BIAS MAP HEATMAPS

We render each bias map as a colour image where blue means below-average and red
means above-average, clipped to ±2 standard deviations. This makes subtle
patterns visible to the human eye. Looking at these side by side across five scenes
for the same sensor gives an immediate visual sense of whether the fingerprint is
consistent.

SCENE INDEPENDENCE GRID

A grid of coloured squares showing the 5×5 Pearson r matrix for each sensor.
Deep green means r close to 1 (consistent fingerprint across scenes), red means
low correlation.

SENSOR SIMILARITY HEATMAP

A 14×14 grid showing how similar every pair of sensors is, on the same green-red
colour scale. Sensors that are highly similar to each other will show bright green
cells; sensors that are uncorrelated will be closer to red.

DENDROGRAM

A tree diagram that visualises the hierarchical clustering result. Sensors that
merged early (at the bottom of the tree) are very similar; sensors with long
branches before they merge are different from everything else.

PSD PLOTS

Two line charts — one for the row direction, one for the column direction — with
one line per sensor. If several sensors share a frequency spike, it stands out
as multiple lines peaking at the same point.

FPN SUMMARY BAR CHART

A simple bar chart showing mean RMS FPN per sensor, labelled with the camera model.
At a glance you can see which sensors have stronger or weaker fixed-pattern noise.

TEMPORAL STABILITY

A comparison between the variance measured from the 10 normal noisy frames and the
variance from the 20 extra-noisy frames. This checks whether our statistics are
consistent between the two noise regimes, which would support the idea that we are
measuring something real about the sensor rather than a chance artefact of the
particular noise realisation we chose.

THE ANALYSIS RULES WE SET FOR OURSELVES
-----------------------------------------

Research like this can easily go off the rails if you start applying processing
steps that change what you are measuring. We wrote down a set of explicit
restrictions at the start:

  - No denoising before measuring anything
  - No ISP operations (white balance, sharpening, demosaicking, tone curves)
  - No normalising or rescaling pixel values for analysis
  - No converting to RGB colour space
  - No training machine learning models
  - No inferring scene content
  - No speculating about physical causes without supporting evidence

Everything is measured on the raw, unmodified sensor values exactly as they came
out of the phone. This keeps the analysis clean and reproducible, and it means
the fingerprints we find are real hardware characteristics — not artefacts of
our processing choices.


---

PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER
------------------------

The pipeline runs in four sequential steps:

  Step 1 → compute_statistics.py    (measure the data)
  Step 2 → extract_bias.py          (extract the fingerprints)
  Step 3 → analyse_bias.py          (compare sensors, cluster, run PSD)
  Step 4 → visualise.py             (turn everything into figures)

Each step reads the output of the previous one, so you can re-run any single
step without redoing the whole pipeline. All intermediate data is saved as
compressed .npz files in the results/ directory.

There is also a utility script (decode_and_save.py) that converts the raw .npz
frames into ordinary PNG images for quick visual inspection. It is not part of
the analysis pipeline — it is just for looking at the data.

WHY THIS MATTERS
-----------------

Understanding sensor fingerprints has several practical uses:

1. CAMERA CALIBRATION — Knowing a sensor's fixed-pattern noise lets you subtract
   it from future images, making the images cleaner without any deep learning.

2. FORENSICS — Sensor fingerprints survive JPEG compression, cropping, and even
   some editing. Researchers use them to determine which camera captured a given
   image, which has legal applications.

3. DENOISING RESEARCH — The AIM 2025 challenge was about building denoising
   algorithms for low-light RAW video. Understanding what part of the noise is
   random versus fixed is directly useful for designing better denoisers. A
   perfect denoiser would separately handle random shot noise (which varies
   frame to frame) and fixed-pattern noise (which stays constant).

4. SENSOR ARCHAEOLOGY — Sometimes old RAW files surface without metadata. By
   comparing the fingerprint of an unknown image against a database of known
   sensor fingerprints, you can determine which physical camera captured it —
   hence the name of this project: Neural Archaeology.


---

WHAT THIS RESEARCH DOES NOT DO (YET)
--------------------------------------

  - It does not try to remove the fingerprints from images
  - It does not train any machine learning models
  - It does not build a fingerprint database for matching
  - It does not analyse the colour information in the Bayer pattern separately

These are natural next steps, but they require moving beyond the strict no-ISP,
no-learning protocol that governs the current study.
